# USB-C receptacle

## What it is

A USB-C receptacle is the female socket mounted on a circuit board. It accepts the reversible oval USB-C plug used for charging and data on most modern consumer electronics.

On a device, the receptacle is usually soldered directly to the main board. It has many small pins arranged in two rows, plus larger pads or through-hole "legs" that provide mechanical strength.

## What it is used for

- Charging the device
- Data transfer to a computer
- Audio and video output (on some devices)
- The most common point of physical failure on portable electronics

## Beginner-safe first use

1. **Diagnose first.** Plug in a known-good cable. Wiggle gently. If charging only works at one angle, the receptacle is likely cracked or has cold solder joints.
2. **Inspect with magnification.** Look for bent pins, cracked plastic, or lifted pads.
3. **Do not attempt replacement unless you have:**
   - a temperature-controlled soldering iron
   - flux and solder wick or a desoldering pump
   - magnification (loupe or microscope)
   - the correct replacement part for your exact device model
4. **Practice on a dead board first.** USB-C receptacles are small; mistakes lift pads and ruin the board.
5. **If the device is under warranty or valuable, consider a professional repair.**

## Safety / stop conditions

- **Disconnect the battery** before soldering on any device. Shorting a lithium battery can cause fire or thermal runaway.
- **Do not use a high-wattage uncontrolled iron.** Excessive heat lifts copper pads off the board.
- **Stop if you see the plastic housing melting or pads lifting.** Let the board cool and reassess.
- **If you are not confident in your soldering skill, stop.** A lifted pad often makes the repair uneconomical.
- **Work in a ventilated area.** Flux fumes and melted plastics are not healthy to breathe.
