# Heat gun

## What it is

A heat gun is a handheld electric tool that blows a stream of very hot air through a nozzle. It looks like a bulky hair dryer but produces temperatures far above what a hair dryer can reach — typically 100 °C to 600 °C depending on the setting.

Common types:

- **Basic fixed-temperature** — one or two speed/heat settings
- **Variable temperature** — dial or digital control for precise heat
- **Hot-air rework station** — benchtop unit with precise airflow and temperature, used for surface-mount soldering

## What it is used for

- Shrinking heat-shrink tubing over wire joints
- Softening adhesive to open glued device housings
- Removing stickers and labels
- Pre-heating a circuit board before soldering
- Drying moisture from components (with extreme caution)
- Loosening thread-locking compound on screws

## Beginner-safe first use

1. **Choose the right nozzle.** A wide nozzle spreads heat; a narrow nozzle concentrates it.
2. **Start at the lowest temperature setting.** You can always add more heat; you cannot un-melt plastic.
3. **Hold the gun 5–15 cm away** and keep it moving. Do not aim at one spot.
4. **Test on scrap first.** Learn how quickly your material reacts before touching the actual workpiece.
5. **Let the tool cool on its stand** after use. The nozzle stays hot for minutes.

## Safety / stop conditions

- **Never point a heat gun at skin or flammable materials.** Serious burns and fires happen quickly.
- **Stop immediately if plastic starts to char, bubble, or emit smoke.** You are too hot or too close.
- **Do not use on lithium batteries.** Overheating a battery can cause fire or explosion.
- **Work in a ventilated area.** Heated adhesives and plastics release fumes.
- **Do not leave a running heat gun unattended.**
- **Wear safety glasses.** Small parts or molten plastic can pop free under heat.
- **If the workpiece contains a battery, remove it first or do not use the heat gun.**
